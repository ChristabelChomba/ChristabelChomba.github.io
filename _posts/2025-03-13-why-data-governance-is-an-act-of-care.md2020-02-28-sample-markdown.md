When people hear *data governance*, they usually picture spreadsheets. Metadata catalogs. Audit logs. Policy documents that live in shared drives no one opens.

They are not wrong. All of those things are part of it.

But I want to make a different argument — one that I have arrived at through years of building governance frameworks in environments where the stakes were not abstract.

**Data governance is an act of care.**

---

## What Is Data Actually About?

Every dataset is a population of people. Every record is a person — their name, their transaction history, their location, their behaviour, their identity. When data is poorly governed — when it is inaccurate, inaccessible, unprotected, or misused — it is not a technical failure. It is a failure to care for the people that data represents.

I learned this at Autochek Africa, where I built the company's data governance framework from the ground up. We were operating across multiple African markets, handling financial data for thousands of customers. There was no framework when I arrived. No policies. No documentation. No clear accountability for who could access what, for what purpose, under what conditions.

What that meant in practice was: people's financial data was being handled without the protections those people would have expected and deserved. Not because anyone was malicious. Because no one had built the systems of care.

---

## Governance as Protection

The Kenya Data Protection Act of 2019 is often framed as a compliance burden — something organisations must do to avoid fines. And yes, the enforcement machinery is real. But this framing misses the point.

The Act exists because Kenyans have a right to know how their data is being used. A right to access it. A right to correct it. A right to object to its processing. These are not technical requirements. They are rights.

When I build a data governance framework, I am not just filling compliance checkboxes. I am building the systems that make those rights real. I am creating the conditions under which an ordinary Kenyan can trust that an organisation is handling their data honestly.

That is care.

---

## Why This Matters Especially in Africa

Africa's data governance landscape is uneven. Large, sophisticated organisations — particularly those with international investors or cross-border operations — are increasingly taking data governance seriously. The regulatory environment is tightening. The reputational cost of breaches is rising.

But most organisations — small and medium businesses, government departments, NGOs, community organisations — are operating without any governance framework at all. And these are often the organisations handling the most sensitive data: health records, financial profiles, land ownership data, identity documents.

The people whose data they hold are often the most vulnerable: rural communities, low-income populations, women who depend on microfinance services, patients at community health centres.

If data governance becomes the exclusive domain of well-resourced organisations, then data protection becomes a privilege. And the communities most at risk from data misuse are the ones least protected.

## What I Am Building Toward

Mama AI exists at this intersection: technical rigour and community consciousness. The belief that getting data governance right is not just about organisational efficiency or legal compliance — though it is also those things. It is about whether the people whose data you hold can trust you with it.

This blog will be a space where I work through these ideas — practically and critically. You will find analysis of the Kenya DPA, frameworks for responsible AI in African contexts, honest reflections on what it takes to build governance programmes in under-resourced environments, and the broader question of what it means for African communities to have genuine data rights.
