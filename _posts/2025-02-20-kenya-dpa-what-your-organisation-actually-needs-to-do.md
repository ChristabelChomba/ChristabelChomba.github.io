The Kenya Data Protection Act came into force in November 2019. It has now been in effect for over five years.

Most Kenyan organisations are still not compliant.

This is not particularly surprising. Compliance frameworks take time to embed, enforcement has been gradual, and the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner (ODPC) has been building its capacity and its track record. But the regulatory environment is tightening, and the organisations that have not taken the DPA seriously are accumulating risk.

This post is a plain-language guide to what the DPA actually requires, what is commonly missed, and where a non-compliant organisation should start.

---

## What the DPA Covers

The Kenya Data Protection Act applies to **any organisation that processes personal data** — that is, any data relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. If you hold customer names, employee records, patient information, financial data, location data, or any other information that can identify a living person, the DPA applies to you.

It applies to organisations based in Kenya. It also applies to foreign organisations that process data **about Kenyan residents**, even if the organisation has no physical presence in Kenya. This is Kenya's equivalent of GDPR's extraterritorial reach.

---

## The Core Principles

The DPA sets out eight data protection principles. Every piece of compliance work flows from these:

1. **Lawfulness, fairness, and transparency** — you must have a lawful basis to process personal data and be transparent about how you use it
2. **Purpose limitation** — data collected for one purpose cannot be used for a different, incompatible purpose
3. **Data minimisation** — collect only what you actually need
4. **Accuracy** — take reasonable steps to keep personal data accurate
5. **Storage limitation** — do not keep personal data longer than necessary
6. **Integrity and confidentiality** — protect data against unauthorised access, loss, or destruction
7. **Accountability** — be able to demonstrate that you comply with all of the above

## What Is Commonly Missed

In my work with organisations across Kenya, these are the gaps I encounter most frequently:

**No lawful basis documented.** The DPA requires that you have a lawful basis for processing personal data — consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests. Many organisations have simply never documented what their lawful basis is for any of their data processing activities.

**Privacy notices that do not meet the standard.** The DPA requires that you inform data subjects about who you are, why you are processing their data, who you share it with, how long you keep it, and what their rights are. Most Kenyan privacy policies either do not exist or are copy-pasted from a foreign template with no relevance to the actual organisation.

**No data subject rights process.** Data subjects in Kenya have the right to access their data, to correct inaccurate data, to object to certain processing, and in some cases to request deletion. Most organisations have no documented process for handling these requests.

**Unregulated data sharing.** Many organisations share personal data with third parties — vendors, partners, contractors — without data processing agreements in place. This is a significant gap.

**No registration with the ODPC.** Data controllers and processors above certain thresholds are required to register with the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner. Many have not.

## Where to Start

If your organisation has done nothing on DPA compliance, here is a practical starting sequence:

1. **Data mapping** — understand what personal data you hold, where it comes from, where it goes, and how long you keep it. You cannot comply with what you do not understand.

2. **Identify your lawful bases** — for each category of data processing, document the lawful basis you rely on.

3. **Update or create your privacy notice** — ensure it covers everything the DPA requires and actually reflects your organisation's practices.

4. **Register with the ODPC** — check whether your organisation is required to register and do so.

5. **Build a data subject rights process** — create a simple procedure for handling access requests, correction requests, and objections.

6. **Review your third-party relationships** — identify all vendors and partners who access your personal data and put data processing agreements in place.

This is not a trivial undertaking. But it is also not as overwhelming as it looks when you approach it systematically.
